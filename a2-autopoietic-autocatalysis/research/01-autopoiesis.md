**Status:** Active research note
**Sources:** Maturana & Varela (1973, 1980), Varela et al. (1974), Varela (1979), Luisi (2003), McMullin (2004), Mingers (1995), Luhmann (1984), Briscoe & Dini (2010), Bianchini (2023)

## 1. Core Definition

### 1.1 The Formal Definition

Maturana and Varela coined the term "autopoiesis" (from Greek *auto-* "self" and *poiesis* "creation, production") in their 1973 work *De Maquinas y Seres Vivos*, later published in English as part of *Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living* (1980). The canonical definition:

> "An autopoietic machine is a machine organized (defined as a unity) as a network of processes of production (transformation and destruction) of components which: (i) through their interactions and transformations continuously regenerate and realize the network of processes (relations) that produced them; and (ii) constitute it (the machine) as a concrete unity in space in which they (the components) exist by specifying the topological domain of its realization as such a network."
>
> --- Maturana & Varela, 1973

This definition encodes three interlocking requirements:

1. **Self-production of components.** The system is a network of processes that produces the very components that participate in those processes. Everything in the system is both producer and product. The enzymes of a cell's metabolism produce the enzymes, as well as the lipids, nucleic acids, and other molecules that constitute the cell.

2. **Realization of the network.** The components, through their interactions, continuously regenerate and realize the network of processes (relations) that produced them. The network does not exist abstractly --- it is concretely realized through the components it produces.

3. **Boundary constitution.** The components constitute the system as a concrete unity in space by specifying the topological domain of its realization. The boundary is not imposed from outside --- it is generated by the very processes the system contains. The cell membrane does not merely enclose life; the life process produces the membrane as its own boundary.

### 1.2 Operational Closure

Operational closure is the defining relational property of autopoietic systems. It means that the system's processes refer only to themselves and the components they produce, forming a self-contained unity. The system is "closed" not in a thermodynamic sense (it is open to flows of matter and energy) but in an *organizational* sense: the network of production processes forms a closed loop.

Key aspects:
- The operations of the system produce only elements of the system itself
- Every process in the network is enabled by other processes in the same network
- The system's identity is constituted through this closure, not through external specification
- The nervous system, for example, maintains its organization, structure, and integrity in the face of perturbations from the environment --- its operations refer to its own states, not to an external "representation" of the world

Operational closure does **not** mean isolation. The system is thermodynamically open (energy and matter flow through it) but organizationally closed (its production processes form a self-referential loop).

### 1.3 The Canonical Example: The Cell

The eukaryotic cell is the paradigmatic autopoietic system:
- **Components:** nucleic acids, proteins, lipids, organelles, cytoskeleton
- **Network of processes:** metabolic pathways that transform molecules, synthesize proteins, replicate DNA
- **Boundary:** the cell membrane, composed of lipids produced by the cell's own metabolism
- **Closure:** the metabolic network produces the components (enzymes, structural proteins, lipids) that constitute and maintain the metabolic network itself, including the membrane that bounds it

The cell does not produce something "other than itself." It produces *itself*. This is the core distinction from allopoietic systems.

## 2. Key Properties

### 2.1 Organization vs. Structure

This is one of the most important and frequently misunderstood distinctions in autopoietic theory.

**Organization** refers to the *relations between components* that define a system's identity --- the abstract pattern of relations that makes a system the kind of system it is. Organization is invariant for as long as the system exists as that type of system.

**Structure** refers to the *actual components and their specific relations* that realize the organization at any given moment. Structure is variable --- it can change while organization remains the same.

Analogy: "Organization" is like the idea of "airplane" (lift-generating surfaces, propulsion, control systems in specific relations), while "structure" is a specific Boeing 747 (particular aluminum alloys, specific engines, concrete wiring). Many different structures can realize the same organization.

For autopoietic systems:
- The **autopoietic organization** is what makes a system living --- the circular, self-producing network of processes
- The **structure** is the specific molecular composition at any instant
- A cell can change every molecule in its body (and does, continuously) while maintaining its autopoietic organization
- If the organization is lost, the system ceases to exist as that type of entity (death, for a cell)
- Structural changes are continuous; organizational change is binary (exists or does not)

### 2.2 Structural Coupling

Structural coupling describes how an autopoietic system interacts with its environment (medium) without losing its organizational autonomy.

> "We speak of structural coupling whenever there is a history of recurrent interactions leading to the structural congruence between two (or more) systems."
>
> --- Maturana & Varela, 1973, p. 75

Key properties:
- An autopoietic system exists embedded in a medium (environment)
- The system and its medium undergo mutual structural changes through their recurrent interactions
- These interactions trigger structural changes in the system, but the system's *own structure* determines what changes occur --- the medium does not specify or determine the change
- Over time, system and medium develop structural congruence --- they "fit" together through a history of mutual perturbation
- Structural coupling is reciprocal: the system perturbs the medium as the medium perturbs the system
- The system maintains its autopoietic organization throughout --- if coupling causes loss of organization, the system is destroyed (disintegration)

Structural coupling explains adaptation and evolution without recourse to representationalism. The organism does not "represent" its environment internally; it develops structural congruence with it through a history of interaction.

### 2.3 Perturbation vs. Instruction

Maturana categorically rejected the notion of "instructive interaction" --- the idea that an external agent can specify or determine the internal state of an autopoietic system.

**Perturbation:** The environment can *trigger* or *select* a change of state in the system, but it does not *determine* what that change will be. The system's own structure at the moment of interaction determines the change. The environment is a source of perturbations, not instructions.

**Structural determinism:** The nature of changes in the organism is determined by its own internal structure, not by the structure of the perturbation. Two structurally different organisms receiving the same perturbation will undergo different changes.

This has profound consequences:
- There is no "information transfer" from environment to system in the instructive sense
- Learning is not the "reception" of information but a structural change triggered by perturbation and determined by the system's own structure
- Language is not an instructive interaction that passively moves information between entities, but a mutual orientation of participants toward a common consensus domain
- The observer cannot instruct the observed system --- only perturb it

### 2.4 The Cognitive Domain (Santiago Theory of Cognition)

Maturana and Varela's theory of autopoiesis led directly to a radical theory of cognition, known as the "Santiago theory of cognition" (named by William Irwin Thompson after the Santiago school where Maturana and Varela worked).

Core claims:
- **Living systems are cognitive systems.** Cognition is not a special faculty added on top of life; it is coextensive with the process of living itself.
- **Living as a process is a process of cognition.** The domain of all interactions in which an autopoietic system can enter without losing its autopoietic organization constitutes its "cognitive domain."
- **Cognition is effective action.** Cognition is not representation of an independent world; it is the system's effective operation in its domain of existence. "All doing is knowing and all knowing is doing."
- **The nervous system does not "represent" the world.** It operates as a closed network of changing relations of activity between its components. Perception is not the reception of information but the specification of a domain of interactions.

The Santiago theory rejects the computational-representational theory of mind. Cognition is not computation over representations of an external world; it is the enactment of a world through a history of structural coupling.

The cognitive domain of a system is defined by the totality of interactions it can undergo while maintaining its autopoietic organization. Different organisms have different cognitive domains --- not because they represent the world differently, but because their structures allow different domains of interaction.

## 3. The Autopoietic Organization: Necessary and Sufficient Conditions

### 3.1 Varela's Six-Point Criteria

Varela, Maturana, and Uribe (1974, "Autopoiesis: The organization of living systems, its characterization and a model," *BioSystems* 5(4): 187--196) proposed six key points for determining whether a system is autopoietic. These function as a decision procedure applied by an observer:

1. **Determine, through interactions, if the unity has identifiable boundaries.** If yes, proceed. If not, the entity is indescribable and nothing further can be said.

2. **Determine if there are constitutive elements of the unity** --- components that can be identified and described. If yes, proceed. If not, the unity is an unanalyzable whole and not an autopoietic system.

3. **Determine if the unity is a mechanistic system** --- whether the component properties are capable of satisfying certain relations that determine in the unity the interactions and transformations of these components. If yes, proceed.

4. **Determine if the components that constitute the boundaries of the unity are produced by the interactions of the components of the unity** --- either by transformation of previously produced components, or by transformations and/or coupling of non-component elements that enter the unity through its boundaries.

5. **Determine if the components of the boundaries of the unity are produced by the interactions of the components of the unity** through processes that operate within the boundary.

6. **Determine if all the components of the unity are produced by the interactions of components within the unity** --- that is, that the components are the necessary and sufficient products of the system's own processes, and that these components in turn are necessary and sufficient to produce the system.

### 3.2 The Six Properties (Condensed)

Varela et al. also gave six properties considered necessary and sufficient to recognize an autopoietic system:

| Property | Description |
|---|---|
| **Bounded** | The system is demarcated from its environment by an identifiable boundary |
| **Complex** | There are distinguishable components within the boundary |
| **Mechanistic** | The system is driven by energy dissipation; component interactions follow deterministic relations |
| **Self-differentiated** | The boundary is intrinsically produced by the system's own processes |
| **Self-producing** | The system produces its own components through internal processes |
| **Autonomous** | The self-produced components are necessary and sufficient to produce the system |

All six must hold simultaneously. Failure of any one means the system is not autopoietic.

### 3.3 Distinguishing Autopoietic from Related Systems

**Autopoietic vs. Allopoietic systems:**
An allopoietic system produces something *other than itself*. A car factory uses raw materials to generate cars --- organized structures that are something other than the factory. An autopoietic system produces *itself*. The cell's metabolic network produces the components of the metabolic network, including its own boundary.

**Autopoietic vs. merely self-organizing systems:**
Self-organization is a broader category. A hurricane is self-organizing (it maintains its structure through energy dissipation) but not autopoietic (it does not produce the air molecules that constitute it; it does not produce its own boundary). Autopoiesis requires self-production of components and self-production of the boundary.

**Autopoietic vs. dissipative structures (Prigogine):**
Dissipative structures (Benard cells, chemical oscillators) maintain organization through energy flow but do not produce their own components or boundaries. They are far-from-equilibrium structures, but they are not self-producing in the autopoietic sense. Zeleny noted that autopoiesis adds the requirement of component self-production and topological boundary self-generation to what dissipative structures achieve.

**Autopoiesis and autonomy:**
Varela (1979, *Principles of Biological Autonomy*) argued that autopoiesis is the mechanism by which living systems achieve autonomy. An autonomous system is one whose identity is determined by its own internal processes rather than external specification. Autopoiesis is the specific form of autonomy characteristic of living systems. Varela later generalized to a broader class of "autonomous systems" that are operationally closed and self-organizing, with autopoiesis as a special case.

## 4. Software Analogies: Autopoiesis and Computational Systems

### 4.1 Computational Autopoiesis: The Varela-Maturana-Uribe Model

The first computational model of autopoiesis predates the field of Artificial Life by over a decade. Varela, Maturana, and Uribe (1974) created a 2D cellular automaton simulation (implemented in FORTRAN IV) in which:

- **Substrate particles** move randomly on a grid (analogous to precursor molecules in a cell's environment)
- **Catalyst particles** can bond substrate particles into **link particles** (analogous to enzymes catalyzing the formation of membrane components)
- **Link particles** bond to each other to form chains, creating a **membrane** around the catalyst
- The membrane selectively prevents substrate entry, creating a bounded unity
- When the membrane is ruptured, the catalyst produces new links to repair it

This model demonstrated spontaneous emergence of a bounded, self-repairing unity --- a minimal autopoietic system in a computational medium. McMullin (1997, 2004) rediscovered and corrected the original algorithm, noting that an important self-repair interaction had been lost in published descriptions.

McMullin's review ("Thirty Years of Computational Autopoiesis," *Artificial Life* 10(3), 2004) concluded that the research program "has generated substantive demonstrations of progressively richer, lifelike phenomena, has illuminated conceptual and methodological problems in the field, and continues to provide an effective framework for addressing key open problems in artificial life."

### 4.2 Towards Autopoietic Computing

Briscoe and Dini (2010, "Towards Autopoietic Computing," *Springer*) reviewed the main concepts of autopoiesis and discussed how they could relate to fundamental concepts and theories of computation. Their mapping:

| Biological Concept | Proposed Software Analogue |
|---|---|
| Cell membrane | System boundary / API surface / container |
| Metabolic network | Process network / service mesh / runtime |
| Component production | Code generation / service instantiation |
| Operational closure | Self-contained module with internal dependency resolution |
| Structural coupling | Event-driven interaction with environment |
| Perturbation | External input / API call / signal |
| Self-repair | Self-healing / auto-recovery / restart policies |

They noted that biological properties of self-organization, self-replication, self-management, and scalability are all addressed by autopoiesis, and argued these properties are desirable for next-generation distributed computing systems.

### 4.3 NASA Autonomic Autopoiesis (GSC-TOPS-97)

NASA Goddard Space Flight Center developed a patented technology (GSC-TOPS-97) for "Autonomic Autopoiesis" in multi-agent systems:

- **Self-managing agents** that are self-configuring, self-healing, self-optimizing, and self-protecting
- **Autopoietic property (self-creation):** when an agent self-destructs (due to security breach or other factors), the system auto-generates a replacement agent
- The replacement agent need not be a clone of the original --- it can be a functionally equivalent alternative
- Includes a "self-esteem reward system" where agents with higher performance trust clone at greater rates

This is one of the few deployed systems explicitly invoking autopoiesis in its design.

### 4.4 Luhmann's Social Autopoiesis and Software Organizations

Niklas Luhmann (1984, *Social Systems*) controversially extended autopoiesis from biology to social systems:

- Social systems are **not** composed of people but of **communications**
- Communications produce further communications, forming operationally closed networks
- Each social system (law, economy, science, politics) produces its own elements (communications) by its own operations
- Systems are structurally coupled to their environment but operationally closed

Relevance to software: Luhmann's framework has been used to analyze software organizations and socio-technical systems. A software system embedded in an organization can be viewed as structurally coupled to human communication systems. However, Maturana himself argued this extension was conceptually unsound, as it ignores that communications presuppose human communicators.

### 4.5 Recent Work: Autopoiesis of the Artificial (Bianchini, 2023)

Bianchini (2023, "Autopoiesis of the artificial: from systems to cognition," *BioSystems*) assessed the advantages and disadvantages of employing autopoiesis for artificial systems:

- Modern AI systems can perform cognitive tasks, but their internal workings remain largely uninterpretable
- Autopoietic organization could provide a principled framework for understanding system identity, boundary, and self-maintenance in artificial systems
- The paper argues for a strong complementarity between temporal and non-temporal phenomena in autopoietic organization
- Autopoietic networks can be modeled as attractors of a dynamic system that tend to self-organize

### 4.6 What Breaks: Where the Analogy Fails

The mapping from biological autopoiesis to software systems has several fundamental problems:

1. **The physicality problem.** Maturana insisted that autopoiesis applies only to systems that produce their components in physical space. Software does not produce its own physical substrate. A running program does not produce the silicon, copper, and electricity that constitute it. The "components" of software (data structures, processes, modules) exist in an abstract computational space, not physical space. Maturana would say these are *simulations* of autopoiesis, not instances of it.

2. **The production problem.** In a cell, the metabolic network literally produces the molecules that constitute the network. In software, a process may instantiate another process, but it does not "produce" the CPU instructions, memory cells, or network interfaces that execute that process. There is always an external substrate (hardware, OS, runtime) that the software depends on but does not produce.

3. **The boundary problem.** A cell's membrane is produced by the cell's own metabolism. A software system's boundary (container, network namespace, API surface) is typically defined by an external system (orchestrator, OS, developer). Self-generating boundaries in software are rare and typically shallow (e.g., a firewall rule generated by the application still depends on external network infrastructure).

4. **The closure problem.** True operational closure requires that every component is produced by processes within the system. Software systems almost universally depend on external components (libraries, OS services, hardware drivers) they did not produce. No software system is operationally closed in the autopoietic sense.

5. **The destruction problem.** Autopoiesis includes "destruction" as part of the production network --- components are continuously degraded and replaced. Software components do not spontaneously degrade. Memory is garbage-collected, processes are killed, but these are imposed by an external runtime, not by the system's own operational dynamics.

6. **The observer problem applied to software.** In autopoietic theory, the observer who distinguishes the system is external to the system. But software systems are designed by observers (developers) who specify the organization in advance. The organization of a software system is not emergent from its component interactions but imposed by design.

## 5. Critiques and Limitations

### 5.1 The Circularity Critique

The most persistent critique of autopoiesis is that it is circular or tautological:

- The definition of autopoiesis defines a phenomenological domain (the autopoietic unity) which then excludes all descriptions irrelevant to that unity. Critics argue this is a "patent circularity" --- the theory presupposes what it claims to explain.
- The system is defined as self-producing, but "self" is defined as what the system produces. Where does the explanatory work happen?
- Defenders respond that this circularity is precisely the point: autopoiesis is *constitutively circular*. The circularity is not a flaw in the explanation but the fundamental property being described. Life *is* constitutive circularity.

### 5.2 The Extension Problem: The Maturana-Varela Disagreement

Maturana and Varela ultimately disagreed about whether autopoiesis could be extended beyond biology:

- **Maturana's position (conservative):** Autopoiesis applies strictly to living systems in physical space. Extending it to social systems, cognitive systems, or software systems strips the concept of its meaning. Maturana explicitly and vocally opposed Luhmann's appropriation of autopoiesis for social systems.

- **Varela's position (liberal):** Varela was more open to generalization. In *Principles of Biological Autonomy* (1979), he developed the broader category of "autonomous systems" --- operationally closed, self-organizing systems --- of which autopoietic systems are a special (biological) case. This opened the door to applying the *organizational logic* of autopoiesis (operational closure, self-production, boundary generation) to non-biological domains, even if the specific autopoietic mechanism (molecular self-production in physical space) does not transfer.

- **The practical consequence:** If Maturana is right, autopoiesis is a theory of life and nothing else. If Varela is right, the organizational pattern of autopoiesis can inspire (but not literally describe) non-biological systems. Most applications of autopoiesis to software, social systems, and AI follow Varela's more liberal interpretation.

### 5.3 Critiques from Mainstream Biology

Autopoiesis has not been widely adopted in mainstream biology:

- Biologists generally find the definition too abstract and philosophically laden to be operationally useful
- The theory does not generate testable predictions in the way that molecular biology, genetics, or evolutionary theory do
- The emphasis on organizational closure seems to underplay the role of genetic information, evolutionary history, and ecological context
- The rejection of "information" as a concept is seen as throwing the baby out with the bathwater --- molecular biology has been enormously productive using informational language (genetic code, transcription, translation)
- Luisi (2003) argued that the difficulty in understanding autopoiesis "lies in its refined conceptual subtlety" rather than vacuity, but conceded that the original definitions are ambiguous enough to have generated decades of confusion

### 5.4 The Observer Problem

Autopoietic theory places the observer in a paradoxical position:

- All descriptions of autopoietic systems are made by observers external to the system
- But observers are themselves autopoietic systems, subject to the same conditions of operational closure
- The observer cannot access the system's "internal" states directly --- only perturb it and observe the consequences
- This leads to a radical epistemological position: all knowledge is the product of an autopoietic system's own operational closure, not a representation of an observer-independent reality
- Critics argue this leads to solipsism or radical relativism. If all knowing is operationally closed, how can we make any claims about systems other than ourselves?
- Maturana's response: objectivity is "put in parentheses" --- we can make coherent descriptions, but we cannot claim they correspond to an observer-independent reality

### 5.5 Luisi's Reappraisal (2003)

Pier Luigi Luisi ("Autopoiesis: a review and a reappraisal," *Naturwissenschaften*, 2003) provided the most detailed critical analysis:

- The original definitions of autopoiesis are ambiguous and have been interpreted in contradictory ways
- Autopoiesis is **not** an abstract theory, **not** a concept of artificial life, **not** a theory about the origin of life --- it is a pragmatic blueprint of life based on cellular life
- Luisi proposed a more precise definition that links autopoiesis to concepts of minimal life, self-organization, emergence, biological autonomy, auto-referentiality, and environmental interaction
- He related autopoiesis to closed systems, boundaries, homeostasis, self-reproduction, causal circularity, organization, and multicellularity
- His core argument: autopoiesis identifies something real and important about living systems (constitutive self-production), but the formulation needs to be sharpened to be scientifically useful

Luisi later (2012, "Autopoiesis 40 years Later: A Review and a Reformulation," *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*) revisited the concept again, proposing further refinements.

### 5.6 Mingers' Analysis

John Mingers (1995, *Self-Producing Systems: Implications and Applications of Autopoiesis*) provided a comprehensive critical examination:

- Thoroughly analyzed whether autopoiesis can be meaningfully applied to social systems
- Concluded that treating social systems as literally autopoietic is problematic but that the concept "compels reconsideration of the role and nature of communication"
- Argued that the theory's value lies in its organizational insight (self-production, operational closure, structural coupling) rather than in its literal applicability to non-biological domains
- Identified that extending autopoiesis to non-physical domains causes the concept of operational closure to collapse, because there is no physical self-production of components

### 5.7 Zeleny's Reinterpretation

Milan Zeleny offered a different interpretation:

- Defined "production" in more general terms than Maturana and Varela
- Treated "autonomy" as fully observer-dependent
- Recognized "topological boundary" as pertaining to structure rather than organization
- Argued that all biological autopoietic systems are essentially social systems --- societies of interacting components --- making autopoiesis and social systems fundamentally interrelated
- This interpretation opens the door wider to non-biological applications but at the cost of diluting the original concept

## 6. Synthesis: Implications for Self-Producing Software

### 6.1 What Transfers

The following organizational principles from autopoiesis are potentially applicable to software system design:

1. **Operational closure as a design principle.** A system whose internal processes produce all the components needed for its own operation, with external dependencies minimized to raw resources (compute, storage, network) analogous to the matter/energy flow through a cell.

2. **Self-produced boundaries.** The system generates and maintains its own interface to the external world --- its API surface, network policies, access controls --- as products of its internal processes, not as externally imposed configurations.

3. **Organization/structure separation.** The system's invariant identity (organization) is the pattern of relations between its production processes. The concrete components (structure) can be continuously replaced, upgraded, or regenerated without loss of identity.

4. **Structural coupling over instruction.** The system interacts with its environment through perturbation, not instruction. External events trigger internal state changes that are determined by the system's own structure, not by the content of the external event.

5. **Constitutive circularity.** The system's components produce the processes that produce the components. This is the fundamental design challenge: achieving genuine circular self-production rather than mere self-monitoring or self-healing.

### 6.2 What Does Not Transfer

1. Physical self-production of substrate (the system will always depend on hardware/OS it did not produce)
2. True thermodynamic openness with organizational closure (software does not metabolize)
3. Spontaneous emergence of organization (software organization is designed, not emergent)
4. Component degradation as a feature (software does not spontaneously decay; entropy must be artificially introduced or simulated)

### 6.3 The Key Design Question

The central question for autopoietic software is: **Can a software system be designed such that its running processes produce the components (code, configuration, data structures, interfaces) that constitute and maintain those very processes?**

This is distinct from:
- Self-healing (detecting and recovering from failures --- reactive, not constitutive)
- Self-configuration (adjusting parameters --- does not produce components)
- Self-optimization (improving performance --- does not produce components)
- Code generation (producing code from templates --- production is not circular)

True autopoietic software would need to achieve *constitutive self-production*: the running system produces its own code, its own runtime structures, its own boundary interfaces, and these products are what enable the system to continue running and producing.
